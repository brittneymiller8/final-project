Bird Loss in Alachua County

You probably see birds every day and probably don't think much about it. But did you know that bird populations are slowly decreasing across the continent?

In September, the Cornell Lab of Ornithology released the first widespread analysis of bird population changes across the U.S. and Canada (Source: Cornell Lab of Ornithology). They found a nearly 3 billion deficit in bird population numbers since 1970 (Source: Cornell Lab of Ornithology).

Rex Rowan, a 45-year bird watcher and long-time member of the Alachua Audubon Society, said that Alachua County is not immune to this decline. He could list several bird species he has seen local declines in, including the northern bobwhite, the loggerhead shrike, the American kestrel, the common nighthawk and the burrowing owl.

The chart above was compiled from data from the Christmas Bird Counts in Gainesville from 1992-95 (Source: Alachua Audubon Society CBC). This was the greatest span of local data found on its website. There was no data about the common nighthawk and the burrowing owl.

Alachua County is a hub of ornithology because of its various protected lands. The county's many avid bird watchers can submit their findings on a popular bird database called eBird. The top eight separate locations pinned on the map below are the top eight separate birding locations as reported on the eBird database (Source:eBird Alachua County Hotspots. Click on each pin to see the number of bird species reported there.
